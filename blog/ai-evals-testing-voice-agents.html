Why Evals Matter for Voice AI

Voice AI agents are fundamentally different from traditional software - they're non-deterministic, conversation-based systems where small errors can cascade into complete failures. Without proper evaluation systems, you're flying blind, deploying agents that might fail in ways you can't predict or measure.

The stakes are high in voice applications. Unlike text interfaces where users might tolerate occasional mistakes, voice interactions demand near-perfect reliability. A single misheard word or inappropriate interruption can destroy user trust instantly.

Key insight: Teams that implement systematic evals see 3-5x faster improvement in their voice agents' success rates compared to those relying on ad-hoc testing. The difference comes from being able to measure progress and focus on fixing what actually matters.

The 3 Types of AI Evaluations

Effective voice agent evaluation requires combining multiple approaches:

1. Unit Tests

Rule-based checks for specific failure modes like:

  • Preventing system prompt leaks
  • Validating correct format for phone numbers
  • Ensuring no profanity in responses

2. LLM-as-Judge

Using AI to evaluate more subjective aspects like:

  • Conversational flow quality
  • Appropriateness of responses
  • Success in achieving conversation goals

Critical: Always measure judge agreement with human experts before relying on automated evaluations.

3. Production Monitoring

Tracking real-world metrics like:

  • Task completion rates
  • User satisfaction scores
  • Escalation to human agents

The #1 Rule: Look at Your Data

The most important (and most skipped) evaluation practice is direct examination of conversation traces. Generic metrics often miss the specific failure modes that matter most for your application.

While tools like Langsmith and BrainTrust provide trace viewing capabilities, experts recommend building custom visualization tools tailored to your specific needs. These can be surprisingly simple to create with modern AI-assisted development.

Pro tip: A basic custom trace viewer can be built in under 30 minutes using AI coding assistants. The time investment pays off immediately in faster error detection and resolution.

Building Effective Test Cases

Your test cases should mirror real-world usage while targeting known pain points:

Happy Path Tests

Core scenarios your agent must handle perfectly:

  • Basic information requests
  • Common transactional flows
  • Frequent user intents

Hill Climbing Cases

Challenging but important scenarios to improve:

  • Complex multi-step requests
  • Interruptions and corrections
  • Edge case handling

Regression Tests

Previously fixed bugs that must stay fixed:

  • Specific failure patterns
  • Customer-reported issues
  • Security vulnerabilities

Using LLM-as-Judge Effectively

LLM judges can accelerate evaluation but require careful implementation:

Do:

  • Validate against human judgments first
  • Use specific, binary criteria when possible
  • Provide clear examples of good/bad responses

Don't:

  • Rely on vague scoring (e.g., 0-100 ratings)
  • Use judges without measuring their accuracy
  • Assume judges understand your domain without training

For voice agents, consider adding audio-specific evaluation criteria like pronunciation quality and appropriate pacing.

Voice-Specific Challenges

Voice agents introduce unique evaluation complexities:

Cascading Failures

Errors in speech-to-text can derail even perfect LLM logic. Your evals must track performance across the entire pipeline.

Latency vs Interruptions

Faster responses may lead to more accidental interruptions. Finding the right balance requires careful measurement.

Alpha-Numeric Accuracy

Numbers, codes, and specialized terms are particularly challenging for voice systems. Dedicated tests are essential.

Real-world example: One team discovered their agent was reading hashtags aloud (#) because they weren't filtering them from LLM responses. This was only caught by direct examination of audio traces.

Key Takeaways

Building reliable voice agents requires a disciplined approach to evaluation that goes beyond standard software testing practices. By implementing systematic evals, you can catch failures before users do, measure real progress, and build trust in your voice AI systems.

In summary: 1) Look at your data directly, 2) Combine multiple evaluation methods, 3) Build voice-specific test cases, and 4) Continuously measure and improve. Teams that follow these practices ship voice agents that users actually trust and rely on.
