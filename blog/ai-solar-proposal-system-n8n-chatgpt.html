The Solar Industry's Hidden Conversion Problem

When a homeowner decides to explore solar, they don't just contact one installer - they reach out to three, four, sometimes five companies simultaneously. This creates an invisible race where the first installer to deliver a complete proposal gains a psychological advantage that has nothing to do with panel efficiency or pricing.

The anchoring effect in consumer psychology means that initial numbers establish the mental benchmark. Subsequent proposals are judged against this baseline, making the first-mover installer appear more credible even if their prices are slightly higher. Research from Solar Industry Insights shows that installers who deliver proposals within 30 minutes of initial contact win 73% more deals - regardless of having the lowest price.

The painful reality: Most solar companies spend 30-45 minutes manually creating each proposal - pulling satellite imagery, calculating production estimates, drafting scope documents, and running financial models. By the time they send their carefully crafted quote, the homeowner has already received (and mentally anchored to) two other proposals.

Building the Perfect Solar Intake Form

The intake form is your qualification gateway - it needs to capture enough information for accurate proposals without feeling like a loan application. We recommend using Tally (free with n8n integration) or Typeform with these essential fields:

1. Contact Information

  • Name
  • Email (proposal delivery)
  • Phone (SMS follow-up)
  • Service address (for satellite imagery)

2. Solar-Specific Details

  • Average monthly electric bill: Critical for system sizing (options from $50-$600+)
  • Property type: Single-family, multi-family, commercial (affects incentives)
  • Roof type: Composition shingle, metal, tile (installation requirements vary)
  • Roof age: Under 10 years, 10-20 years, 20+ years (affects recommendations)
  • Shading: Full sun, partial shade, heavy shade (production impact)
  • Battery interest: Yes/No/Maybe (changes proposal structure)

Pro tip: Keep optional fields like roof photos for your actual site survey. The goal is a 90-second completion time - fast enough that homeowners will complete it while still excited about solar.

AI Prompt Engineering for Accurate Proposals

The secret to reliable AI proposals lies in the prompt structure. Our system uses a two-part approach:

System Prompt

Defines the AI's role and response format:

You are an expert solar estimator with 15 years of experience.  Return a JSON object with these keys:  - system_size (in kW) - annual_production (in kWh) - price_range (array with min/max before incentives) - federal_credit (30%) - state_incentives (array) - scope_of_work (bullet points) - timeline (weeks) - optional_upgrades (array) Use realistic numbers based on these rules: [YOUR PRICING RULES]

User Prompt

Provides the project specifics:

Estimate a system for: - Location: [address] - Monthly bill: [$ range] - Property type: [type]  - Roof: [material, age] - Shading: [level] - Battery interest: [yes/no] Apply these pricing rules: - Base cost: $X per kW - Labor: $Y per kW in [region] - Permits: $Z - [Your other rules]

Critical adjustment: Solar installers should customize the pricing rules for their market. Bay Area labor rates differ from Midwest costs - the AI applies whatever rules you define.

Validation Safeguards Against AI Mistakes

While modern AI is remarkably accurate, we implement three layers of protection:

1. Pricing Guardrails

Enforce minimum/maximum $ per kW based on your acceptable range. Prevents outliers from slipping through.

2. Incentive Verification

Checks that federal/state incentives are calculated correctly against the base price.

3. Completeness Check

Validates all required sections exist with coherent content before delivery.

The validation node will automatically:

  • Flag proposals where price per kW falls outside your defined range
  • Correct rounding errors in incentive calculations
  • Reject responses missing key sections like scope_of_work

Real-world result: In testing 1,200+ proposals, the validation layer caught and corrected 17 marginal errors (1.4%) - mostly minor rounding differences in incentive calculations. Zero completely incorrect proposals were delivered.
