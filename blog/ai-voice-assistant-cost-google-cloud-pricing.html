The 4 Cost Buckets Explained

To conquer cost uncertainty, we break Google Cloud AI pricing into four measurable buckets that cover everything from listening to responding:

1. Speech-to-Text Conversion

This is your voice input meter running. Every minute of audio processed gets charged, whether it's a 10-second command or a 5-minute monologue. Google charges per 15-second increments, so short frequent interactions add up quickly.

2. AI Processing (Tokens)

The mysterious "tokens" that determine your LLM costs. One token ≈ one word. You're charged for both the words the AI needs to process (input tokens) and the words it generates (output tokens). At 3:40 in the video, we show how a simple "What's the weather?" query can consume 15+ tokens once you account for system prompts.

3. Text-to-Speech Conversion

The voice your users actually hear. Most providers charge either per character or per second of generated audio. High-quality neural voices cost 2-4x more than standard voices.

4. Infrastructure Costs

The nuts and bolts: compute instances, networking, storage. While typically smaller than the first three buckets, these become significant at scale.

Cost distribution benchmark: For a typical voice assistant, expect 40% of costs in AI processing (tokens), 30% in speech-to-text, 20% in text-to-speech, and 10% in infrastructure.

Why Fixed Pricing is Dangerous

The most common mistake developers make is quoting a flat monthly fee for AI voice projects. This creates a dangerous disconnect where your revenue stays constant while costs scale with usage. At 5:10 in the video, we show the profit crossover point where actual costs exceed your flat quote.

This isn't just theoretical - one client we worked with lost $12,000 in three months because their $5,000/month flat fee couldn't cover unexpected usage spikes. The solution? Shift from product thinking to service thinking with tiered pricing that mirrors how phone companies structure plans.

The Phone Company Pricing Model

Mobile carriers don't offer unlimited everything forever - they create tiered plans with clear usage limits and overage charges. This same model works perfectly for AI voice assistants:

Basic Plan ($299/month)

  • 500 minutes of speech processing
  • 250,000 tokens (input + output)
  • Standard voice output
  • $0.10/minute overage

Professional Plan ($999/month)

  • 2,000 minutes of speech processing
  • 1 million tokens
  • Neural voice option
  • Priority support
  • $0.08/minute overage

Implementation Checklist

To build your accurate pricing model, you need concrete assumptions about how your voice assistant will be used. Follow this checklist before writing a single line of code:

1. Usage Volume

  • How many daily active users do you expect?
  • What's the average number of interactions per user per day?

2. Interaction Patterns

  • What's the average audio input length? (e.g., 8 seconds for commands vs 30+ seconds for dictation)
  • How verbose are the AI responses? (token count)

3. Operational Parameters

  • Will this run 24/7 or business hours only?
  • How many languages need support?
  • What voice quality is required?

Pro tip: Build a simple spreadsheet that multiplies your assumptions by Google's published rates. The video at 9:15 shows our template that automatically calculates cost per user and required pricing tiers.
