Make.com Automation Overview

This automation solves the invoice transfer problem by connecting Google Sheets directly to Zoho Invoice through Make.com. When a record's status changes to "ready to invoice," the workflow automatically:

  1. Pulls the customer and product details from Google Sheets
  2. Verifies the customer exists in Zoho (as shown at 9:20 in the video)
  3. Matches products to Zoho's item list
  4. Creates a complete invoice with all line items
  5. Updates the spreadsheet status to "invoice sent"

The entire process happens without human intervention, running on a schedule you set (daily by default, but configurable to hourly or real-time). At 6:30 in the tutorial, you'll see the initial scenario setup in Make.com that makes this possible.

Step 1: Setting Up the Google Sheets Trigger

The automation begins by monitoring your Google Sheets invoice log for records marked "ready to invoice." At 7:45 in the video, you'll see the exact "Search Rows" module configuration that makes this work:

Critical configuration: The query status = 'ready to invoice' must match your spreadsheet's status column exactly (case-sensitive). The demo uses column I for status tracking, but you can adapt this to your sheet's structure.

When testing this module (shown at 8:10), you'll see how Make.com returns only records matching your criteria. This selective triggering ensures the automation only processes invoices that are truly ready, ignoring draft or pending records.

Step 2: Customer Matching in Zoho

Before creating an invoice, the automation must verify the customer exists in Zoho and retrieve their contact ID. At 11:30 in the tutorial, you'll see the "List Contacts" module that queries Zoho's customer database.

The key to reliable matching is using consistent customer identifiers between systems. The demo matches on company name (as shown at 12:10), but you could use email, custom IDs, or other unique fields depending on your data structure.

Pro tip: For businesses where customers might not exist in Zoho yet, you can extend this workflow with a "Create Contact" module that automatically adds new customers before invoice creation.

Step 3: Product Mapping Logic

Just like customers, products must be matched between systems. At 13:45 in the video, the "List Items" module queries Zoho's product catalog to find matches for each line item from Google Sheets.

The demo assumes products already exist in Zoho with matching names (shown working at 14:30), but you could modify this to:

  • Use SKUs or product IDs instead of names for more reliable matching
  • Create missing products automatically
  • Apply default products when matches aren't found

This product mapping is crucial for accurate invoice line items with correct pricing and tax treatment in Zoho.

Step 4: Invoice Creation Module

With customer and product data prepared, the workflow creates the actual invoice in Zoho. At 16:20 in the tutorial, you'll see the "Create Invoice" module configuration that ties everything together.

The critical fields to map include:

  • Contact ID (from the customer matching step)
  • Item IDs (from product matching)
  • Quantities (from your Google Sheets)
  • Invoice date (using Make's now function or your sheet's date field)
  • Invoice number (using your spreadsheet's ID or a generated sequence)

When testing this module (shown at 17:40), you'll see the invoice appear in Zoho exactly as if created manually - but with perfect accuracy and instant completion.

Step 5: Automated Status Updates

To complete the cycle, the workflow updates your Google Sheets record to "invoice sent" (or your preferred status). At 20:10 in the video, you'll see the "Update Row" module that makes this happen.

This status tracking serves three important purposes:

  1. Prevents duplicate invoice creation for the same record
  2. Provides visibility into which invoices have been processed
  3. Creates an audit trail of automation activity

The demo shows updating a simple status field (at 21:30), but you could extend this to include the Zoho invoice number, creation timestamp, or other metadata for complete tracking.

Critical Error Handling Considerations

At 22:40 in the tutorial, you'll see what happens when the automation encounters missing data - a common real-world scenario. The demo fails when a customer isn't found in Zoho, highlighting the need for proper error handling.

In a production implementation, you'd want to:

  • Log failed records to a separate sheet for follow-up
  • Send notifications to your accounting team
  • Implement conditional logic to handle partial data
  • Create fallback processes for edge cases
