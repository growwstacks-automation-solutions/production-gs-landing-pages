The Data Challenge Amazon Didn't Solve

Imagine requesting your purchase history from a major platform, only to receive a 2GB zip file containing 20 folders, multiple subfolders, and nearly 1,800 scattered data files - with zero documentation about where anything is or what format it's in. This was the reality when Amazon provided a Kindle purchase history export.

The user wanted a simple list of all Kindle books ever purchased, with titles, authors, purchase dates and genres. What they got was an undocumented data dump requiring hours of manual exploration just to understand the structure.

Key Insight: Most platforms prioritize data export compliance over usability. The growing "right to access" movement means businesses increasingly receive - and need to process - similarly messy data exports from vendors, partners and customers.

How Standard Chatbots Failed (One By One)

The test compared four approaches to this data challenge: ChatGPT (free version), Google Gemini (free), Perplexity (Pro), and Claude (Pro). The results revealed critical limitations in current chatbot capabilities for real data engineering work.

ChatGPT's free version could only see one folder in the zip file, missing 95% of the data. When asked to extract book details, it produced a spreadsheet with transposed columns and mostly blank fields for authors and genres - essentially useless output.

Google Gemini failed immediately with a "too many files to process" error. Perplexity Pro couldn't even accept the zip file, rejecting it with a "file type not supported" message. Claude Pro performed best among chatbots, but still had significant data quality issues requiring manual cleanup.

Grading the Chatbots: Claude Pro scraped a D for at least getting book titles right. ChatGPT free earned an E for partial zip access. Gemini and Perplexity received Fs for complete failure to process the file.

Claude Code's Winning Approach

Running locally on a Linux environment, Claude Code took a fundamentally different approach to the problem. Rather than trying to process everything at once like the chatbots, it:

  1. Recursively explored all folders and subfolders to map the complete data structure
  2. Identified the ASIN (Amazon Standard Identification Number) as the common identifier across files
  3. Automatically handled deduplication of records appearing in multiple files
  4. Generated and executed a Python script to extract and merge the required fields
  5. Produced a clean CSV with only one missing author (for a compendium) and one missing genre

When the user mistakenly referred to "ISIN" instead of "ASIN", Claude Code politely corrected them while still understanding the intent - a small but telling example of its contextual awareness.
