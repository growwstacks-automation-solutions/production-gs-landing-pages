Why Your Company Knowledge Matters for AI

Generic AI models like ChatGPT have a critical limitation - they only know what was publicly available during their training. Your proprietary business knowledge - product details, internal processes, customer data - remains invisible to them. This creates a dangerous gap when you try to automate business processes with AI.

The solution? Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). This approach combines the power of large language models with your specific business knowledge. At 4:30 in the video, the speaker demonstrates how even simple recipe questions fail when the AI lacks access to the right knowledge base.

Critical insight: AI without your business knowledge is like a new hire who never got proper training - they might sound confident but will make costly mistakes when handling your specific processes and data.

RAG Architecture Explained

RAG solves the knowledge gap through a two-phase process. First, it retrieves relevant information from your knowledge base. Then, it uses that context to generate accurate, grounded responses. The video at 7:15 shows this workflow visually in n8n.

Key components include:

  • Indexing pipeline: Prepares your knowledge for retrieval (documents, databases, APIs)
  • Vector database: Stores information by meaning rather than exact matches
  • Retrieval logic: Finds the most relevant knowledge for each query
  • Augmented generation: Combines retrieved knowledge with the LLM's capabilities

This architecture ensures your AI applications answer based on your actual business knowledge rather than generic information.

Implementing Naive RAG in n8n

The simplest RAG implementation (called "naive RAG") involves three n8n workflow steps shown at 12:30 in the video:

Step 1: Knowledge Indexing

Create a workflow that:

  1. Retrieves data from your sources (database, documents, APIs)
  2. Chunks the content into manageable pieces
  3. Generates vector embeddings using an embedding model
  4. Stores these in a vector database like Weaviate

Step 2: Query Processing

When a question comes in:

  1. Convert the query to a vector
  2. Find the most similar knowledge chunks
  3. Pass these to the LLM as context

Step 3: Response Generation

The LLM generates an answer grounded in the retrieved knowledge. At 15:45, the video shows how this works for recipe questions.

Implementation tip: Start with this simple approach to understand RAG fundamentals, but be aware of its limitations covered in the next section.

Common RAG Challenges and Solutions

Naive RAG often fails in production due to several key issues demonstrated at 18:20:

1. Context Loss in Chunking

When documents are split too small, critical context disappears. The video shows how this leads to nonsense answers like "spaghetti with wooden pancakes."

Solution: Add metadata (document IDs, titles) to chunks and adjust chunk sizes based on content type.

2. Hallucination Without Grounding

When no relevant knowledge is found, the LLM may invent answers (19:50 in video).

Solution: Strengthen system prompts to refuse ungrounded answers and implement guardrail workflows.

3. Semantic Search Limitations

Pure vector search struggles with numeric filters (like "recipes under 10 minutes").

Solution: Combine semantic search with traditional filtering (shown at 22:30).

Key metric: In our implementations, these solutions reduce incorrect answers by 60-80% compared to naive RAG.

Advanced RAG Techniques

For production systems, we implement more sophisticated approaches shown at 25:10:

1. Metadata Enrichment

Use AI during indexing to extract and add structured metadata (dates, categories, numeric values). This enables hybrid search combining semantic and traditional filters.

2. Agentic Retrieval

Instead of fixed retrieval logic, let the AI decide how to search based on the question. At 27:45, the video shows an agent choosing between different retrieval tools.

3. Multi-hop Reasoning

Complex questions may require multiple retrieval steps with refined queries - the agent can decide when this is needed.

These techniques make RAG systems more flexible and accurate for real business use cases.

AI Evaluation - The Missing Piece

At 32:40, the video highlights why evaluation is critical - AI behavior changes over time and between model versions. Without proper testing, you won't notice degradation.

Our evaluation approach in n8n involves:

  1. Creating a test dataset with questions and ideal answers
  2. Running these through your RAG system
  3. Using another LLM to compare actual vs expected answers
  4. Calculating metrics (accuracy, helpfulness, grounding)

This automated evaluation lets you:

  • Compare different model versions
  • Test indexing changes
  • Monitor production performance

Enterprise requirement: For mission-critical applications, we recommend evaluating at least 200-500 test cases to get reliable metrics.

Vector databases store information by meaning rather than exact text matches. They use embedding models to convert text into numerical vectors that capture semantic relationships.

When you query the database, it finds content with similar meaning to your question, enabling more relevant knowledge retrieval for your AI system compared to traditional keyword search.

  • Embeddings capture conceptual meaning
  • Similar vectors = similar concepts
  • Enables "search by meaning" not just keywords

Naive RAG simply chunks and indexes documents, often leading to poor results. Advanced RAG adds metadata enrichment, better chunking strategies, hybrid search (combining semantic and keyword search), and sometimes agentic approaches.

In agentic RAG, the AI can decide how to retrieve information based on the question context, potentially using multiple retrieval methods or refining its search based on initial results.

  • Naive RAG has fixed retrieval logic
  • Advanced RAG adapts to question needs
  • Agentic approaches can chain multiple retrievals
