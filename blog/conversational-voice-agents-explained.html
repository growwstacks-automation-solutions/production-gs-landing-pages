What Are Conversational Voice Agents?

Traditional chatbots revolutionized customer service by automating text-based interactions. But typing queries still creates friction - users must navigate to a chat interface, formulate their question in writing, and wait for a response. Conversational voice agents remove these barriers by enabling natural voice interactions.

Imagine calling your bank and having an AI assistant that understands spoken requests like "What's my account balance?" or "Transfer $500 to savings." This is the power of voice agents - they combine speech recognition, natural language processing, and speech synthesis to create human-like conversations.

Key difference: While chatbots require typing, voice agents process natural speech in real-time, making interactions faster and more accessible - especially for older users or those less comfortable with technology.

How Voice Agents Work: Architecture Breakdown

At 4:32 in the video, the instructor diagrams the complete voice agent pipeline. Unlike simple chatbots, voice agents require four tightly integrated components working in perfect harmony:

  1. Speech-to-Text (STT)

    Converts spoken audio into text using models like Moonshine STT. Advanced voice activity detection (VAD) determines when the user starts and stops speaking.

  2. Language Model (LLM)

    Processes the transcribed text using models like GPT-4. This is where conversation logic, knowledge retrieval, and response generation happens.

  3. Text-to-Speech (TTS)

    Converts the LLM's text response back into natural sounding speech using synthesis models like Coqui TTS.

  4. Streaming Infrastructure

    Connects all components with low-latency to enable real-time back-and-forth conversations without awkward pauses.

Critical insight: Commercial platforms hide this complexity behind simple APIs, but understanding the architecture helps you optimize costs and performance when building custom solutions.

These platforms become prohibitively expensive for high-volume use cases like:

Four core components work together: speech-to-text conversion (STT), large language model processing (LLM), text-to-speech synthesis (TTS), and real-time streaming infrastructure.

These must be tightly integrated with low latency to create natural conversations. The streaming component is particularly critical to avoid awkward pauses between turns.

  • STT: Moonshine, Whisper
  • LLM: GPT-4, Claude, etc.
  • TTS: Coqui, VITS
  • Streaming: FastRTC, WebRTC

Custom solutions using FastRTC become cost-effective at higher volumes (8+ hours daily) and when you need control over models, data privacy, or custom integrations.

FastRTC defaults include Moonshine for speech-to-text and Coqui TTS for text-to-speech, but supports integration with other open-source models.

These models provide good quality while being completely free to use, unlike commercial API-based solutions. You can mix-and-match models based on your quality and language requirements.

  • STT: Moonshine, Whisper
  • TTS: Coqui, VITS
  • VAD: Silero VAD

Streaming processes audio in chunks rather than waiting for complete utterances, reducing latency between user speech and agent response.

This creates more natural conversations where responses begin forming while the user is still speaking, similar to human dialogue patterns. Without streaming, conversations feel stilted with noticeable pauses.

  • Reduces perceived latency
  • Enables overlapping speech
  • Creates fluid conversation flow
