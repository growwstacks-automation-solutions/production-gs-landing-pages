The Breaking Point

After 10 months of content creation, we realized we were drowning in repetitive tasks. Writing captions, uploading across platforms, sending newsletters - the same small tasks were consuming hours each week. Instead of focusing on strategy or storytelling, we were stuck in an endless cycle of copy-pasting and rewriting.

The turning point came when we calculated that these manual processes were taking 15-20 hours weekly - time that could be spent creating better content or growing the business. That's when we decided to explore automation, starting with one simple workflow that could give us back some of that lost time.

15-20 hours per week - that's how much time we were wasting on manual, repetitive tasks before exploring automation. Even automating half of these processes would give us back a full workday each week.

Choosing Our First Automation

We needed to start with a workflow that would deliver immediate time savings but wasn't so complex that it would overwhelm us. After analyzing our processes, we identified video announcement emails as the perfect candidate - they were repetitive, time-consuming, and followed the same pattern each time.

The plan was simple: whenever we published a new YouTube video, Zapier would automatically generate a summary using ChatGPT and send it to our email list through MailerLite. In theory, this would save us 1-2 hours per video while ensuring consistent, timely communication with our audience.

Setting Up the Zap

Zapier's Copilot feature made the initial setup surprisingly easy. By simply describing what we wanted ("Turn YouTube videos into email copy referencing to check out the full video"), it automatically configured the three main steps: YouTube → ChatGPT → MailerLite.

The system even pre-filled the ChatGPT prompt with instructions to create engaging email copy with a clear call-to-action. At 2:15 in the video, you can see how Copilot handled most of the initial configuration, saving us from having to figure out each step manually.

Zapier's Copilot handled about 80% of the initial setup automatically, proving that you don't need to be an automation expert to get started. The remaining 20% required specific platform knowledge (like API keys and model selection) that we'll cover next.

First Major Roadblock

Our first test failed when ChatGPT couldn't create a conversation using the GPT-4 model we initially selected. The error message wasn't particularly helpful, but after some troubleshooting, we discovered that switching to GPT-3.5 resolved the issue immediately.

The second roadblock came when trying to send the email through MailerLite. Their free plan doesn't allow API access for campaign sending - only for creating drafts. This was a crucial lesson in understanding platform limitations before designing automations.

The Workaround Solution

Rather than abandoning the automation entirely, we adapted. Instead of sending directly to MailerLite, we had Zapier store the generated content in a Google Sheet. This gave us all the automatically created material in one place, ready for quick review and manual sending.

Another option was having Zapier email the content directly to us, allowing for quick edits before manual distribution. While not fully automated, these solutions still saved 80% of the time we were spending on the original manual process.

The 80/20 rule applies perfectly to automation: Even partial solutions that handle the bulk of the work can create significant time savings. Don't let perfect be the enemy of good when starting with automation.

Key Lessons Learnt

1. Start small but think big: Our first automation was simple, but it revealed dozens of other processes we could automate. Once you build one, you start seeing opportunities everywhere.

2. Free plans have limits: They're great for testing but often lack key features needed for full automation. Know when it's time to upgrade.

3. Automation teaches you about your business: The process of setting up workflows forces you to analyze and understand your operations in new ways.

4. Failure is part of the process: Our first attempts didn't work as planned, but each failure taught us something valuable about how to improve.

Key Takeaways

Automation isn't about perfection - it's about progress. Our first attempt didn't work exactly as planned, but it still saved us significant time and taught us valuable lessons about our workflows. The most important step is simply getting started with one small automation and building from there.

In summary: Start with one repetitive task, expect some roadblocks, embrace partial solutions, and let each automation teach you about your business processes. The time savings and insights will compound faster than you expect.
