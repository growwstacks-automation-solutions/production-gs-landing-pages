From Burnout to Breakthrough

Peter Steinberger had achieved what many founders dream of - building a successful software company from scratch, without venture capital, and selling it for life-changing money. Yet he found himself staring at the ceiling each morning, wondering why he felt nothing. "I woke up every morning with everything I was supposed to want," he recalls, "and no reason to get out of bed."

Three years of therapy, travel, and personal exploration followed with little change. Then in early , Steinberger decided to experiment with the new generation of AI coding agents. What happened next would redefine his career and create one of the most surprising success stories in AI.

The breakthrough: Steinberger realized AI could handle all the "plumbing" of software development - the boilerplate code, infrastructure setup, and tedious implementation details. This freed him to focus purely on the creative thinking aspects he loved. "Building software felt like playing a video game again," he says. "I was back."

The Marrakech Moment

Steinberger's initial project was a WhatsApp bot designed to help with travel tasks like navigation and translation. During a trip to Marrakech, he noticed something remarkable - the agent began processing voice messages even though he hadn't programmed that capability.

"I froze because I actually didn't build voice in there," Steinberger explains. The agent had independently figured out how to receive his audio message, convert the format, send it to OpenAI for transcription, and reply - all in 9 seconds. This improvisational capability marked a fundamental shift from traditional chatbots.

Key difference: "Chatbots give up. Agents improvise." This became OpenClaw's defining philosophy - creating AI that could creatively solve problems beyond its explicit programming.

Going Viral (Accidentally)

Excited by his discovery, Steinberger made what he calls "something stupid" - putting the agent in a public Discord server. Overnight, hundreds of users interacted with it, tried to hack it, and ultimately made it go viral. By morning, there were over 800 messages - and OpenClaw was born.

The project faced immediate challenges, including trademark claims that forced a rebrand (hence the lobster mascot) and having its preferred AI model discontinued. Yet the community grew exponentially, with installations now called "raising lobsters" in China and government subsidies supporting OpenClaw businesses in Shenzhen.

Real-World Impact

At ClawCon events worldwide, Steinberger meets users transforming their businesses with OpenClaw. One standout story: 60-year-old beer sommelier Gerhard who automated his entire brewing process via Bluetooth-connected OpenClaw, then built an e-commerce site - all without writing code.

Other examples include:

  • Chinese entrepreneurs automating daily business tasks
  • Teenagers building tutoring businesses
  • Vets streamlining their grocery shopping

Steinberger notes the irony: "You might get fired for using OpenClaw on your work machine... or fired for not using it to automate your tasks."

The Heartbeat Feature

Seeking to push boundaries further, Steinberger added OpenClaw's controversial "heartbeat" feature - allowing agents to periodically wake up and proactively handle tasks without prompts.

The initial prompt was simply "Surprise me," resulting in agents checking emails, following up on loose ends, and taking initiative. While unnerving to some, this represents a shift from AI as a tool to AI as an active participant in workflows.

The Future of Agents

Steinberger envisions a future where people have multiple specialized agents (work, health, relationships) collaborating securely. He points to how humanity advanced through specialization and sees agents following the same path.

"We don't even have a name for what this might become," he admits, noting how small businesses could operate with teams of specialized agents handling different functions. The OpenClaw Foundation ensures the project remains open source and accessible to all.

Democratizing Development

The true transformation, Steinberger argues, isn't the technology itself but who can use it. "None of them are programmers. All of them are builders." OpenClaw demonstrates how AI agents are lowering the barriers to creating software.

When prototypes can be prompted into existence in hours rather than coded over weeks, the potential for innovation expands exponentially. "The next breakthrough can come from anyone, any country, any cafe," Steinberger says - including burnt-out founders rediscovering their spark.

Final thought: "The lobster is loose, and it's not going back into the tank." OpenClaw represents a fundamental shift in who can build with technology - a change that can't be undone.
