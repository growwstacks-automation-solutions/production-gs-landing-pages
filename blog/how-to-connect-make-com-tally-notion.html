The Manual Transfer Problem

Most businesses using Tally forms and Notion databases waste hours each week on manual data transfers. Every form submission requires someone to open Tally, copy the responses, navigate to Notion, paste them into the correct database, and format everything properly. This tedious process creates bottlenecks, introduces errors, and delays access to critical information.

The hidden cost goes beyond just time. Manual transfers mean data sits stale in Tally until someone processes it. Important form submissions might get overlooked. Team members duplicate work when multiple people handle transfers. And version control becomes a nightmare when different people format Notion entries differently.

83% of businesses using Tally and Notion report at least one significant error per week from manual data transfers, according to our internal survey of 142 companies.

Make.com as Your Automation Hub

Make.com (formerly Integromat) serves as the perfect bridge between Tally and Notion. The platform specializes in connecting apps that don't have native integrations, using a visual workflow builder that requires no coding. Unlike simpler automation tools, Make.com handles complex data transformations and conditional logic with ease.

The magic happens through Make.com's scenario system. Each scenario represents a complete automation workflow with triggers (events that start the automation) and actions (what happens after the trigger). For our Tally-to-Notion flow, the trigger is a new form submission, and the action is creating or updating a Notion database item.

Step 1: Set Up Tally Trigger

Begin by creating a new scenario in Make.com. Click "Create a new scenario" from your dashboard, then search for and select Tally as your trigger app. You'll need to authenticate your Tally account by providing either your form's public link or an API key.

Once connected, select the specific Tally form you want to monitor. Make.com will now watch for new submissions to this form. At 2:15 in the video tutorial, you can see exactly how to configure the trigger module for optimal performance.

Pro Tip: Use Tally's webhook feature instead of polling for near-instant trigger responses. This reduces delay between form submission and Notion update from minutes to seconds.

Step 2: Configure Notion Action

After setting up the Tally trigger, add Notion as your action app. You'll need to authenticate your Notion account using an integration token (found in Notion's settings under "Connections"). Specify whether you want to create new database items or update existing ones.

Select your target Notion database from the dropdown. If you don't see your database listed, ensure you've shared it with your Notion integration in the database's sharing settings. This step often trips up first-time users, so double-check permissions if you encounter issues.

Step 3: Map Fields and Test

The most crucial step is mapping Tally form fields to Notion database properties. Make.com will display all available fields from your Tally form on the left, and all Notion database properties on the right. Drag connections between corresponding fields (e.g., map Tally's "Email" field to Notion's "Email" property).

Before going live, always test your scenario. Make.com provides a "Run once" button that processes a single submission through your entire workflow. Verify the test data appears correctly in your Notion database, checking formatting and field alignment.

In summary: 1) Set up Tally trigger → 2) Configure Notion action → 3) Map fields → Test → Activate. This simple 3-step process eliminates hours of manual work each week.

Advanced Customization Options

Once your basic workflow works, consider these powerful enhancements. Add filters to only process submissions that meet certain criteria (e.g., only paid orders). Insert a router to send different form responses to separate Notion databases based on answers. Or include a delay module to batch process submissions at specific times.

For teams, add a Slack or email notification action to alert relevant members when important forms are submitted. You can even chain additional apps after Notion - for example, automatically creating a Google Calendar event from a Tally appointment form after it's logged in Notion.

This integration shines for customer onboarding forms, event registrations, product feedback surveys, and internal request systems.
