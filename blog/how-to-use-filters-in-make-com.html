What Are Filters in Make.com?

Filters are conditional gatekeepers in your Make.com automations. They act as simple yes/no questions about your data that determine whether the next module in your scenario should run. Without filters, every module in your scenario executes sequentially, which can lead to unnecessary operations or incorrect actions when the data doesn't meet your requirements.

Think of filters like airport security checkpoints. Just as security only lets through passengers who meet certain criteria, Make.com filters only allow data that meets your specified conditions to proceed to the next module. This keeps your automations clean, efficient, and accurate.

Key benefit: Filters can reduce your Make.com operation usage by up to 40% by preventing unnecessary module executions when your data doesn't meet the required conditions.

How Filters Work: The Simple Checkpoint System

At their core, filters evaluate a simple true/false condition about your data. When the scenario runs, Make.com checks whether the data passing between modules meets your filter criteria. If it does (true), the next module executes. If it doesn't (false), Make.com skips that module and moves on.

This creates a branching effect in your automations where different paths can be taken based on your data's characteristics. For example, you might have one path for high-value orders (over $100) and another for standard orders, with filters determining which path each order takes through your scenario.

Step-by-Step: Setting Up Your First Filter

Adding a filter to your Make.com scenario is straightforward once you know where to look. Follow these steps to create your first filter:

Step 1: Identify where to place your filter

Filters live between modules in your scenario. Look for the connection line between two modules where you want to add a condition. You'll click this line, not the modules themselves, to access the filter options.

Step 2: Open the filter editor

Click the connection line between your modules. In the panel that appears, select "Set up a filter." This opens the filter editor window where you'll define your conditions.

Step 3: Define your filter condition

The filter editor has three main components we'll explore in detail next. For now, know that you'll select a value to check, choose a comparison operator, and specify what to compare against.

Step 4: Save and test your filter

Once your condition looks right, click Save. Run your scenario with test data that both meets and doesn't meet your condition to verify the filter works as expected.

The 3 Essential Components of Every Filter

Every Make.com filter consists of three key parts that work together to create your condition:

1. The value to check

This is the specific piece of data from the previous module that you want to evaluate. It could be an amount, status, email address, phone number, or any other field available from the preceding module's output.

2. The comparison operator

This determines how Make.com will evaluate your value. Common operators include:

  • Equals/Does not equal
  • Greater than/Less than
  • Contains/Does not contain
  • Exists/Is empty

3. The comparison value (when needed)

For most operators, you'll need to specify what to compare against - a number like 100, text like "approved", or a pattern like "@gmail.com". The exceptions are "exists" and "is empty" which don't require a comparison value.

Remember: Filters evaluate to either true (continue) or false (skip). Your condition must be precise to get the behavior you want.

Practical Filter Examples You Can Use Today

Let's look at some real-world filter examples you can implement immediately in your Make.com scenarios:

Example 1: Process only high-value orders

Condition: Amount > 100
Use case: Only send follow-up emails or trigger special processing for orders over $100.

Example 2: Route emails by domain

Condition: Email contains "@gmail.com"
Use case: Handle Gmail users differently than other email providers in your workflow.

Example 3: Skip incomplete records

Condition: Phone exists AND is not empty
Use case: Ensure you only process records with complete contact information.

Example 4: Handle different status values

Condition: Status equals "Approved"
Use case: Only execute certain actions for approved items, skipping others.

Pro tip: You can combine multiple conditions in a single filter using AND/OR logic for more complex rules.

Pro Tips for More Accurate Filters

After setting up hundreds of filters for clients, we've learned these best practices the hard way:

1. Watch your data types

Make sure numbers are treated as numbers (not text) in comparisons. A text "100" won't evaluate the same as a numeric 100 in greater-than comparisons.

2. Handle text variations

Text comparisons are case-sensitive by default. "Approved" won't match "approved". Consider using the lowercase function in your scenarios for more consistent matching.

3. Name your filters clearly

When you have multiple filters in a scenario, give each one a descriptive name like "Amount > $100" so you can understand your logic at a glance later.

4. Test edge cases

Always test your filters with blank values, unexpected formats, and boundary conditions to ensure they behave as intended in all situations.

Common mistake: Forgetting that "contains" looks for exact substring matches. "gmail.com" won't match "user@gmail.com" - you need "@gmail.com".
