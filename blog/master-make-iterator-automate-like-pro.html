Iterator vs Aggregator: What's the Difference?

Most Make.com beginners struggle with bulk data processing - either drowning in manual repetition or creating inefficient workflows that treat all data the same. The Iterator and Aggregator tools solve these problems in opposite ways.

While the Iterator breaks down bundles into individual items (perfect for personalized emails), the Aggregator combines multiple items into single bundles (ideal for batch processing). Understanding this distinction is crucial for building effective automations.

Key insight: Use an Iterator when you need to process items individually (like sending emails), and an Aggregator when you need to combine items for batch operations (like processing group payments).

Real-World Example: Volunteer Email Automation

A nonprofit organization faced a critical problem - their volunteer signup system wasn't sending personalized welcome emails consistently. Over 18 volunteers had signed up but never received proper onboarding communications.

The solution? A Make.com workflow using Google Sheets as a trigger and the Iterator to process each volunteer individually. The automation identified volunteers with "failed" email statuses and sent customized welcome messages with each person's name and specific signup details.

Implementation tip: Always set processing limits on your Iterator (like 50-100 items) to prevent system overload while still handling your volume efficiently.

How the Iterator Actually Works

The Iterator acts like a precision splitting tool in your automation workflow. When it receives a bundle of items (like rows from a Google Sheet), it systematically breaks them down and feeds them one-by-one to subsequent modules.

In our volunteer example, the Iterator took 18 spreadsheet rows and processed them sequentially through the Gmail module. Each iteration pulled the correct volunteer's information and inserted it into the personalized email template before sending.

Technical note: The Iterator maintains context throughout each cycle, ensuring variables like {{name}} always reference the current item's data, not the entire bundle.

Setting Up Triggers for Your Iterator

Effective Iterator workflows start with smart triggering. In our case, we used a Google Sheets trigger that activated only when the "email status" column showed "failed" - creating a self-healing system for missed communications.

You can configure triggers based on various conditions: new form submissions, specific status changes, or even time-based schedules. The key is ensuring your trigger provides clean, structured data for the Iterator to process.

  1. Connect your data source (Google Sheets, Airtable, etc.)
  2. Set filter conditions to select only relevant items
  3. Define processing limits if needed
  4. Map the output bundle to your Iterator

Tracking Mapping Details Like a Pro

One of Make.com's most powerful features is visible mapping - showing exactly where each piece of data originates in your workflow. This prevents errors and helps troubleshoot issues quickly.

By hovering over mapping details (like the 2:15 mark in the video), you can trace each variable back to its source. In our example, we verified that volunteer names came from column C in the Google Sheet before mapping them to the email template.

Debugging advantage: When an email shows incorrect data, you can follow the mapping trail directly to the source field and correct it at the origin.

When to Use an Iterator in Your Workflows

Iterators shine in scenarios requiring individual processing of bulk data. Common use cases include personalized communications (emails, SMS), individual record updates, and sequential approvals.

They're particularly valuable when you need to: handle exceptions (like failed emails), process new submissions individually, or maintain personalized context throughout a workflow. The alternative - manual processing - often leads to errors and inconsistency.

  • Personalized customer onboarding sequences
  • Individualized invoice generation
  • One-to-one survey follow-ups
  • Customized report distribution
