The Token Crisis Nobody Talks About

Most Claude users hit the same frustrating wall - you're deep in a productive session when suddenly you're out of tokens. What few realize is that the solution isn't buying more capacity, but optimizing how you use what you have through prompt caching.

Claude's caching system allows it to reuse previously processed content at a 90% discount, but most users accidentally break their cache constantly without realizing it. The difference is staggering - proper cache maintenance can extend your effective session limits by 5x or more.

91 million tokens saved in one day: That's what one user achieved simply by understanding and maintaining their Claude cache properly. At normal rates, that would have cost 10x more in tokens.

How Claude's Caching System Actually Works

Claude's cache operates at three levels: system instructions (globally cached), project files (cached per project), and conversation history (cached per session). When you send a message, Claude checks if it matches a cached prefix before processing new content.

The magic happens in the pricing - cached tokens cost just 10% of normal input tokens. So when you see "cache read" in your usage, those tokens are being reused at a massive discount. But this only works if you maintain cache continuity.

At 2:45 in the video, the presenter shows a clear example of how caching builds turn by turn. The first message has no cache, but subsequent messages within the TTL window reuse cached content, only processing new additions.

Real-World Savings: 91M Tokens in One Day

The numbers don't lie - one user saved 91 million tokens in a single day through proper cache maintenance. Over a week, that grew to over 300 million tokens saved. To put that in perspective, 91 million cached tokens only cost the equivalent of processing 9 million fresh tokens.

These savings come from understanding the cache time-to-live (TTL) rules. For Claude Pro subscriptions, the cache lasts 1 hour by default. But if you exceed your weekly limit and enter pay-per-token mode, or if you're using sub-agents, the TTL drops to just 5 minutes - a potential token drain if you're not careful.

Cache creates vs. reads: Your dashboard shows cache creates (writing to cache) and cache reads (reusing cached content). Maximizing reads while minimizing creates is the key to token efficiency.

The 3 Things That Break Your Cache

Understanding what breaks your cache is just as important as knowing how it works. The three main cache-breakers are: waiting longer than the TTL (1 hour or 5 minutes), changing the model (like switching between Opus and Sonnet), and modifying system prompts.

As shown at 7:30 in the video, even using the "Opus plan" model setting (which switches between Opus for planning and Sonnet for execution) breaks cache with each model switch. This explains why some users suddenly burned through tokens faster - they were unknowingly resetting their cache constantly.

The Golden Habit for Cache Maintenance

The single most effective habit for maintaining your cache is simple: keep sessions focused and start fresh when switching tasks. If you pause longer than your TTL (usually 1 hour), begin a new session rather than continuing the old one.

For clean transitions between tasks, use /compact or /clear commands, or better yet, use a session handoff skill that summarizes your progress before starting fresh. The video demonstrates this at 9:15, showing how to hand off a 205,000-token project seamlessly to a new session.

Pro tip: For large documents, use Claude projects rather than chat. Projects have more optimized caching for files and documents compared to pasting content directly into chat.

Common Cache Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Many users make these cache-killing mistakes without realizing it: leaving sessions idle past the TTL, frequently switching models mid-conversation, pasting large documents directly into chat instead of using projects, and making small tweaks to system prompts that force full recaching.

The solution is awareness and better habits. Monitor your token usage to spot when you're breaking cache unnecessarily. Use projects for document-heavy work. And most importantly - when in doubt, start a fresh session rather than reviving an old one past its TTL.
