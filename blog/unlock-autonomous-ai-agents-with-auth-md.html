The Agentic Revolution in Software Development

The way we build software has undergone a seismic shift in the past year. What began as advanced autocomplete has evolved into fully autonomous AI agents capable of writing complete applications. As Michael Grinich explains in his MCP Night keynote, "Software engineering has changed more in the last four months than it has over the last 40 years."

Companies like RAMP and Stripe are already seeing the majority of their pull requests generated by AI agents. At Work OS, their internal Horizon system autonomously responds to Slack messages, Linear tickets, and even Sentry errors - opening PRs to fix issues without human intervention.

Key insight: Modern AI agents don't just write code - they solve problems end-to-end, choosing their own tech stack and deployment platforms. This represents a fundamental shift from human-driven to agent-driven development.

The Critical Registration Problem for Agents

As agentic systems become more capable, they face an ironic limitation - the web's registration systems are designed to block exactly the kind of automated behavior these agents represent. CAPTCHAs, email verification, and form filling present insurmountable barriers to autonomous agents.

"We've spent decades trying to limit the amount of automated behavior on the web," Grinich notes. "Suddenly now we have the opposite problem where we want to let the automated stuff in." Traditional registration flows assume human capabilities that agents simply don't have.

False belief demolition: Many assume agents can simply use existing OAuth flows, but these require human consent at critical points. True agentic autonomy requires registration flows built specifically for non-human entities.

How auth.md Solves Agentic Registration

Auth.md is an open specification that provides the missing primitive for agentic registration. It enables services to declare how agents can register, including what identity proofs are accepted and what authentication flows are supported.

The system is built around several key principles:

  • Automated discovery through a standard /.auth.md endpoint
  • Capability declaration so agents know what a service offers
  • ID JAG tokens for verified identity assertions
  • Flexible permission scopes and entitlements
  • Clear free tier constraints for unpaid usage

Unlike traditional standards, auth.md is intentionally lightweight and focused solely on solving the registration problem. "It doesn't do everything under the sun," Grinich emphasizes. "It just solves that really first kind of hairy problem around agent registration."

Understanding ID JAG Tokens

At the heart of auth.md is the ID JAG (Identity Assertion JWT Authorization Grant) token. This JWT contains verified claims about an agent's identity that service providers can trust.

Key components of an ID JAG include:

  • Email verification status (critical for trust)
  • Issuer (the identity provider)
  • Audience (the service being registered with)
  • Capability declarations

The token enables agents to prove their identity without going through human-centric verification flows. Service providers who recognize the issuing identity service can immediately create accounts based on these verified assertions.

The Emerging Agent Economy

Auth.md represents more than just a technical solution - it enables what Grinich calls "the agent economy." As agents gain the ability to register for and pay for services autonomously, they become independent economic actors.

"Agents will be able to pay for services, use things, and actually transact on the web," Grinich predicts. This creates opportunities for:

  • Services specifically designed for agent consumption
  • New monetization models based on agent usage
  • Agent-to-agent marketplaces and ecosystems

The scale potential is enormous. As Grinich notes, "Every company will have agents and they'll have more agents than people." These agents don't sleep, creating continuous demand for services.

How to Implement auth.md for Your Service

Implementing auth.md involves three key steps:

  1. Create your auth.md file: This declares your agent registration capabilities in a standardized format
  2. Implement ID JAG verification: Accept tokens from trusted identity providers
  3. Design your agent API: Ensure your service's capabilities are exposed in ways agents can consume

The auth.md specification is intentionally simple to lower adoption barriers. Early implementers like Cloudflare and Firecrawl have shown it can be added to existing services without major architectural changes.

Business impact: Being early to implement agentic registration could provide significant first-mover advantage as the agent economy emerges. Salesforce's announcement shows even established players see this as critical.

Future Outlook: Agent-Ready as the New Enterprise-Ready

Grinich draws a parallel between today's need for "agent-ready" services and the earlier shift to "enterprise-ready" that enabled SaaS companies to sell to large organizations.

"Agent-ready is actually the next enterprise-ready," he asserts. Just as enterprise features opened up larger markets, agent capabilities will unlock growth from the emerging AI economy.

The implications are profound:

  • APIs become more important than UIs (as Salesforce acknowledged)
  • Documentation and developer experience must account for agent consumers
  • New monetization models will emerge based on agent usage patterns

Companies that embrace this shift early will be positioned to capture disproportionate value as the agent economy matures.
