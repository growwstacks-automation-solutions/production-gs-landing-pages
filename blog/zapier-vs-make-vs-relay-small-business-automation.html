Zapier: The Industry Standard

Zapier has become synonymous with automation for small businesses, and for good reason. With over 7,000 integrations, it's often the first tool business owners try when looking to connect their apps. The platform shines in its marketing content and template library, offering ready-made solutions for common workflows.

However, Zapier's free plan comes with significant limitations - notably restricting users to two-step automations. This "premium wall" often frustrates solopreneurs who hit this limitation quickly in their automation journey. The platform also uses a task-based pricing model that can become expensive as your automation needs grow.

Key Insight: While Zapier excels at simplicity and breadth of integrations, its pricing structure often pushes small businesses to explore alternatives once they outgrow the basic functionality.

Make (Formerly Integromat)

Make (previously known as Integromat) takes a different approach to automation. Immediately noticeable is its team-oriented interface, suggesting it's designed more for collaborative environments than individual solopreneurs. The platform offers powerful visual programming capabilities through its "scenarios" (equivalent to Zapier's zaps).

Where Make stands out is in handling complex workflows with multiple steps, conditions, and transformations. Unlike Zapier's linear approach, Make allows for branching logic and parallel processes. However, this flexibility comes with a steeper learning curve - particularly when dealing with webhooks and API connections.

Pro Tip: Make's data stores and functions capabilities make it ideal for businesses needing to manipulate data between apps or maintain small databases within their automations.

Relay: The New Contender

Relay enters the market with a fresh approach focused on simplicity and transparency. Its standout feature is the human-in-the-loop functionality, allowing users to pause automations for manual review or approval - perfect for contracts, invoices, or client communications.

Unlike Zapier's restrictive free plan, Relay doesn't limit the number of steps in your automations. The interface is clean and intuitive, with helpful error handling that makes troubleshooting easier for non-technical users. While Relay's integration library is smaller than Zapier's, it covers all major business platforms and continues to expand based on user demand.

Why It Matters: Relay's pricing model is straightforward and predictable - a breath of fresh air for small businesses tired of surprise costs from task-based pricing.

Dashboard and Interface Comparison

Each platform presents a distinctly different user experience. Zapier's dashboard prioritizes discovery and templates, making it easy to find pre-built automations. Make's interface resembles a developer tool with its visual scenario builder and technical options. Relay keeps things simple with a clean workflow list and prominent error notifications.

For small business owners wearing multiple hats, the learning curve varies significantly. Zapier and Relay can be picked up in hours, while Make often requires days to feel comfortable with its advanced features. The choice depends on your technical comfort level and how complex your workflows need to be.

Workflow Complexity and Capabilities

When building the same automation across all three platforms, notable differences emerge in flexibility and control. Zapier's linear approach works well for simple "if this then that" sequences but becomes cumbersome for multi-step processes. Make excels at complex transformations and parallel processing but can feel overwhelming for basic needs.

Relay strikes an interesting balance - offering more flexibility than Zapier without Make's complexity. Its killer feature is the approval step, allowing human intervention in automated processes. This proves invaluable for small businesses handling sensitive documents or financial approvals.

Real-World Example: A simple CRM-to-email workflow that takes 3 minutes to set up in Zapier might require 10 minutes in Relay (with more options) or 30 minutes in Make (with maximum flexibility).

Pricing Breakdown for Small Businesses

Pricing often becomes the deciding factor for small businesses. Zapier's free plan is generous until you hit the two-step limitation, after which costs escalate quickly with task-based pricing. Make offers more generous plans for complex workflows at comparable prices to Zapier.

Relay provides the most transparent pricing without limiting steps in automations. Its entry-level plan includes all features (just with lower usage limits), making it ideal for businesses that want predictable costs. All three offer annual payment discounts, but Relay's structure is generally easiest to budget for.

Integration Availability

Zapier's vast library of 7,000+ integrations is unmatched, covering everything from major CRMs like HubSpot and PipeDrive to niche tools. Make comes second with comprehensive coverage of business apps, while Relay, being newer, focuses on quality over quantity in its integration selection.

For most small businesses, all three platforms cover the essential tools - email services, CRMs, calendars, and document platforms. The difference emerges when connecting to specialized industry-specific software, where Zapier's breadth often wins.

Best Use Cases for Each Tool

After testing all three platforms, clear patterns emerge for ideal use cases:

When to Choose Zapier:

  • You need maximum app integrations
  • Want pre-built templates for common workflows
  • Prefer the simplest possible setup

When to Choose Make:

  • Your workflows require complex data transformations
  • Need parallel processing or advanced logic
  • Have technical team members to manage automations

When to Choose Relay:

  • Your processes require human approval steps
  • Want transparent, predictable pricing
  • Value simplicity without sacrificing functionality

Key Takeaways

After extensive testing, our recommendation for small businesses is clear: start with Relay if you value simplicity and approval workflows, consider Make if you need advanced capabilities, and use Zapier when you require niche integrations.

In summary: Zapier wins on integrations, Make on complexity, and Relay on usability and pricing. Most small businesses will find Relay offers the best balance of features and affordability as they begin their automation journey.
