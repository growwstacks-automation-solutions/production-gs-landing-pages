The Problem

This can result in missed opportunities and reduced profitability.

How It Works — Streamlining Market Analysis

The automated market sizing agent simplifies the process of calculating TAM, SAM, and SOM, providing analysts with accurate and actionable insights.

  1. Data Collection: The workflow begins by collecting market data from various sources, including industry reports, market research databases, and company financials.
  2. Data Cleaning: The collected data is cleaned and preprocessed to remove inconsistencies and errors, ensuring data quality.
  3. Top-Down Analysis: The system performs a top-down analysis, starting with the overall market size and narrowing down to the specific segment of interest.
  4. Bottom-Up Analysis: A bottom-up analysis is conducted, starting with individual sales and scaling up to the total market size.
  5. AI Validation: OpenAI is used to validate the data and calculations using the Rule of Three methodology, ensuring accuracy and reliability.
  6. Excel Population: The validated data and calculations are automatically populated into Excel templates, providing a clear and concise overview of the market landscape.
  7. Notification: Stakeholders are notified via email or Slack when the market sizing analysis is complete, ensuring timely access to critical information.

💡 Data Validation: The Rule of Three methodology ensures that market size estimates are accurate and reliable, reducing the risk of making decisions based on flawed data.

Frequently Asked Questions

Market sizing is estimating the total potential market demand for a product or service. It helps businesses understand revenue opportunities, make informed investment decisions, and prioritize market segments.

Accurate market sizing prevents wasted resources on non-viable markets. It provides a foundation for strategic planning and resource allocation, ensuring that efforts are focused on the most promising opportunities.

There are two primary approaches: top-down and bottom-up. The top-down approach starts with the overall market and narrows down to the specific segment. The bottom-up approach starts with individual sales and scales up to the total market.

Both approaches have strengths and weaknesses, and often a combination is used for validation. The top-down approach is useful for understanding the overall market potential, while the bottom-up approach provides a more granular view of the market.

TAM (Total Addressable Market) is the total market demand for a product or service. SAM (Serviceable Available Market) is the portion of the TAM that a company can realistically reach. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) is the portion of SAM that a company can realistically capture.

Understanding these three metrics is crucial for setting realistic goals. TAM provides a high-level view of the market potential, SAM identifies the reachable market, and SOM sets realistic expectations for market share.

Automating market sizing saves time, reduces errors, and improves accuracy. Automation allows for faster iteration and analysis of different scenarios.

It also frees up analysts to focus on higher-level strategic tasks, rather than manual data collection and calculation. Automation ensures that market sizing is performed consistently and accurately, reducing the risk of making decisions based on flawed data.

AI can be used to automate data collection, clean and validate data, and generate insights. AI algorithms can identify patterns and trends that humans might miss.

AI can also be used to create predictive models for market growth and demand. AI-powered market sizing tools can provide more accurate and timely insights, enabling businesses to make better decisions.

The Rule of Three is a methodology used to validate market size estimates. It involves using three different approaches to estimate the market size and then comparing the results.

If the three estimates are within a reasonable range of each other, it increases confidence in the accuracy of the estimate. This methodology helps to reduce the risk of making decisions based on flawed data.
