Create Your Own Social Media Distribution System

The Problem

Managing multiple social media platforms can quickly become a complex and time-consuming task for any business or individual. The constant need to create, schedule, and publish content across various channels often leads to inefficiencies and increased operational costs.

Each social media platform has its own unique requirements for content, character limits, and media formats, making it challenging to maintain consistency and optimize posts manually. Furthermore, many platforms lack robust native scheduling features, forcing users to rely on third-party tools that can be expensive or require repetitive manual actions to keep content flowing.

The Need

Social media managers are constantly seeking a more streamlined and "set it and forget it" approach to content distribution. The ideal solution would allow for easy adjustment of where content is posted and what specific content is available, without being tied to rigid individual post scheduling.

Such a system would empower users to manage their entire social media presence from a centralized location, ensuring that content reaches the right audience on the right platform at the optimal time, all while minimizing manual effort and maximizing efficiency.

The Solution

The "Social Media Automator" built with Make.com (formerly Integromat) provides an effective answer to these challenges. This custom solution enables automated posting to Facebook Pages, Twitter, and LinkedIn, supporting both personal and business profiles.

The core functionality involves selecting a random post from a Google Sheet and distributing it to one or more specified social networks. This setup utilizes several Make.com modules in conjunction with a Google Sheet, offering a flexible and powerful way to manage social media content.

Setting Up Your Google Sheet

The first step in building this automation is to prepare your content in a Google Sheet. This sheet will serve as the central repository for all your social media posts, allowing for easy management and modification. You'll need to define several columns to capture all the necessary information for each post.

It's recommended to create two separate sheets within your Google Sheet: one for posts that are "to be published" and another for posts that have "already been published." This separation is crucial for preventing duplicate posts and maintaining a clear record of your content distribution.

  • Channel: This column specifies the target social media networks for each post. You can use acronyms like "FB" for Facebook, "TW" for Twitter, and "LI" for LinkedIn. If a post is intended for multiple platforms, you can list all relevant acronyms.
  • Message: This field contains the primary text or introduction that will accompany your post on social media.
  • Link: Include the full URL of any external content you wish to share, such as a blog post, article, or product page.
  • Link Name: This is a shorter, descriptive name for the link, often used by platforms like Twitter that have character limitations.
  • Link Description: A brief description of the linked content, which some social networks display alongside the link name.
  • Hashtag: Optional hashtags that can be added to your message to increase visibility and reach.

Additionally, you will add a third sheet to your Google Sheet document. This sheet will contain a simple function to count the total number of rows or posts available in your "to be published" sheet. Since this function operates with numerical values, posts will be counted based on their unique ID numbers, ensuring an accurate tally for the automation process.

Building the Make.com Scenario

Once your Google Sheet is properly structured, the next step is to configure the automation scenario in Make.com. This involves chaining together several modules to perform specific actions, from fetching content to publishing it and managing its status.

The scenario is designed to run periodically, picking a post, distributing it, and then marking it as published to avoid repetition. This modular approach provides both flexibility and control over your social media content flow.

1. Get the Current Number of Rows

The first module in your Make.com scenario will be a Google Sheets module configured to retrieve the current number of rows from your "to be published" sheet. This is achieved by reading the value from the cell in your dedicated counting sheet that contains the COUNT function.

This step is crucial because it dynamically determines the range from which a post can be selected, ensuring that the automation always works with the most up-to-date list of available content.

2. Set a Random Number for Post Selection

Following the row count, you'll use a "Set Variable" module. This module generates a random number within a specified range. The range should be set from 2 (assuming your first row is for headers) up to the total number of rows obtained in the previous step.

This random number will then be used to select a unique post from your "to be published" Google Sheet, ensuring a varied and non-repetitive content stream across your social media channels.

Configuration for the Google Sheets module to count rows.

3. Retrieve the Post Content

Next, another Google Sheets module, specifically "Get Range Values," will be used. This module retrieves the entire row of data corresponding to the random number generated in the previous step.

By fetching the full row, the automation gains access to all the details of the selected post, including the message, link, link name, description, and hashtags, which are essential for publishing to social media.

Setting up the Google Sheets module to retrieve a specific row.

4. Route Content to Social Networks

A "Router" module is then employed to direct the post content to the appropriate social media platforms. Each route connected to the router will lead to a specific social network module (e.g., Facebook Pages, Twitter, LinkedIn).

Crucially, each route will include a "Filter" module. This filter checks the "Channel" field from your Google Sheet. For instance, if the "Channel" field for a post contains "FB," the filter will allow the data to pass to the Facebook Pages app, ensuring the post is only published on the intended platforms.

An overview of the complete Make.com scenario for social media distribution.

5. Move Published Posts to "Already Published"

After a post has been successfully distributed through the social media modules, it's important to update its status in your Google Sheet. An "Add a Row" Google Sheets module will copy the entire row of the now-published post from the "to be published" sheet to the "already published" sheet.

This action serves as a record-keeping mechanism, allowing you to track which content has already gone live and on which date. It also prepares for the next step in preventing duplicates.

6. Delete Published Posts from "To Be Published"

The final step in the content management process within the Google Sheet is to remove the published post from the "to be published" sheet. A "Delete a Row" Google Sheets module will perform this action, using the row number of the recently published post.

By deleting the row, you ensure that the same post is not inadvertently selected and published again in future runs of the scenario, maintaining a clean and efficient content queue.

Pro tip: To ensure your social media posts are always fresh and relevant, regularly review your "already published" sheet. You can set up a separate automation to periodically archive older posts or reintroduce evergreen content back into the "to be published" queue after a certain period, keeping your content strategy dynamic.

Scheduling the Scenario

To fully automate your social media distribution, you need to set up a schedule for your Make.com scenario. The frequency and timing of this schedule should align with your audience's activity patterns and your content strategy.

For example, NewPath Consulting runs this scenario every 4 hours, between 7 am and 11 pm, Monday through Friday. This schedule is optimized to reach their audience during typical working hours, maximizing engagement. You can customize this schedule to fit your specific needs, whether it's daily, hourly, or at specific times of the week.
