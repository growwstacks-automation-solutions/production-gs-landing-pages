What This Workflow Does

Manually transferring content from a Google Form submission to a WordPress post is a repetitive, time-consuming task that often leads to delays and errors. Whether you're collecting guest posts, testimonials, event details, or product ideas, the process of copying, formatting, and publishing each response can eat up hours of your team's valuable time.

This automated workflow solves that problem entirely. It acts as a silent content assistant, monitoring your Google Form for new responses. The moment someone submits a form, the automation springs into action. It takes the submitted data—like the post title, body content, author name, and category—and uses it to create a perfectly formatted draft or published post in your WordPress site. The result is a seamless pipeline from submission to publication, ensuring your website is always updated with the latest content without any manual effort.

The business value is clear: you reclaim time for strategic work, eliminate the risk of copy-paste mistakes, and dramatically speed up your content lifecycle. This automation is perfect for blogs, news sites, community platforms, or any business that relies on user-generated or regularly submitted content.

How It Works

The automation follows a logical, step-by-step process to bridge the gap between form submission and web publication.

Step 1: Trigger on New Form Response

The workflow is initiated every time a new submission is received in your connected Google Form. Make.com watches the form in real-time, ensuring no response is missed.

Step 2: Parse and Organize Submission Data

The automation extracts the data from each form field. It maps the respondent's answers to the corresponding elements needed for a WordPress post: the form's "Title" question becomes the post title, the "Content" answer becomes the post body, and so on.

Step 3: Format Content for WordPress

Here, the raw text is prepared for the web. The workflow can add basic HTML formatting (like paragraphs or line breaks), assign a category based on a form selection, set tags, and even handle the author's name. This step transforms unstructured form data into a structured blog post.

Step 4: Create the Post in WordPress

Using the WordPress REST API, the automation securely sends the formatted data to your website. It creates a new post, setting its status according to your preference—you can choose to publish immediately or create it as a draft for editorial review.

Pro tip: Set posts to "Draft" initially if you require editorial approval. This gives you control to review and tweak content before it goes live, combining automation's speed with human oversight for quality.

Step 5: Notification & Logging (Optional)

After the post is created, the workflow can send a confirmation email to your team or the submitter, or log the action in a Google Sheet. This provides visibility and completes the automated loop.

What You'll Need

  2. A WordPress website (self-hosted or WordPress.com) with administrator access.
  3. WordPress Application Password (for self-hosted sites) or a connected app password for WordPress.com. This is the secure key that allows Make.com to create posts on your behalf.

Quick Setup Guide

Follow these steps to configure the template for your own use:

  1. Clone the Template: Click "Get This Workflow" and create a copy in your Make.com account.
  2. Connect Google Forms: In the first module, authorize Make.com to access your Google account and select the specific form you want to use.
  3. Connect WordPress: In the WordPress module, add your website's URL and the generated Application Password. Test the connection.
  4. Map Your Data: Match the form response fields (like "1. What's the title?") to the corresponding WordPress post fields (Title, Content, Status, Author, etc.).
  5. Set Publishing Rules: Decide if posts should be "Published" immediately or saved as "Draft." Set the default author and category if needed.
  6. Test & Activate: Turn the scenario on and submit a test entry to your Google Form. Check your WordPress site to confirm the post was created correctly.
