Track Brand Mentions with AI Sentiment Analysis

Automatically monitor online conversations about your brand with AI-powered sentiment analysis. Save hours of manual tracking and get real-time insights.

Download Template JSON · n8n compatible · Free

What This Workflow Does

This automation solves the time-consuming problem of manual brand monitoring. Instead of spending hours searching for mentions across blogs, articles, and online platforms, this workflow automatically scrapes content, identifies brand references, and analyzes sentiment using AI.

The system connects Bright Data for reliable web scraping without restrictions, OpenAI for intelligent sentiment analysis, and Google Sheets for organized data storage. It transforms scattered online conversations into structured, actionable insights about how your brand is perceived.

How It Works

1. Content Collection

The workflow begins by scraping targeted online sources (blogs, articles, forums) using Bright Data's web scraping capabilities. It accesses content without being blocked and collects relevant text for analysis.

2. Brand Mention Detection

AI agents scan the collected content for specific brand keywords and references. The system identifies not just direct mentions but also contextual references and related discussions about your brand or competitors.

3. Sentiment Analysis

OpenAI's language models analyze each mention to determine emotional tone—categorizing content as positive, negative, or neutral. The AI understands context, sarcasm, and nuanced language that simple keyword matching would miss.

4. Data Organization

All findings are structured and saved to Google Sheets with timestamps, sentiment scores, source URLs, and key excerpts. This creates a searchable database of brand mentions over time.

5. Alert Generation

The system can trigger alerts for negative sentiment spikes or significant mentions, enabling rapid response to potential reputation issues before they escalate.

Who This Is For

This workflow is ideal for marketing teams, PR agencies, brand managers, and business owners who need to monitor their online reputation. It's particularly valuable for companies in competitive markets, startups establishing brand presence, or businesses experiencing rapid growth where manual monitoring becomes impossible.

Competitive intelligence teams will find it invaluable for tracking competitor mentions and market perception. Customer success teams can use it to identify pain points and positive feedback from indirect channels.

What You'll Need

  1. n8n instance (self-hosted version required for community nodes)
  2. Bright Data account for web scraping capabilities
  3. OpenAI API key for sentiment analysis
  4. Google Sheets access for data storage
  5. Target URLs or sources to monitor for brand mentions
  6. Brand keywords including product names, company names, and common misspellings

Pro tip: Start with 3-5 high-value sources rather than trying to monitor everything at once. Focus on industry publications, review sites, and forums where your target audience actively discusses brands in your space.

Quick Setup Guide

  1. Download and import the JSON template into your n8n instance
  2. Configure Bright Data credentials in the MCP Client node
  3. Add your OpenAI API key for sentiment analysis
  4. Connect to Google Sheets and create your monitoring spreadsheet
  5. Define target URLs and brand keywords in the configuration nodes
  6. Test with a single source before scaling to multiple monitoring points
  7. Schedule the workflow to run daily or weekly based on your needs

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about brand monitoring automation and sentiment analysis

Automated brand monitoring is essential because online conversations happen 24/7 across countless platforms where your customers and competitors are talking. Manual monitoring misses 80-90% of these mentions, leaving businesses blind to reputation risks and market opportunities.

For example, a negative review on a niche forum can escalate to mainstream social media within hours. Automated systems catch these early signals while manual methods typically discover issues only after they've gained traction.

AI sentiment analysis uses natural language processing models trained on millions of text examples to understand emotional context. Unlike simple keyword matching, these models analyze sentence structure, word relationships, and contextual clues to determine true sentiment.

The technology can distinguish between "This product is sick!" (positive slang) and "This product made me sick" (negative). It also detects sarcasm, mixed emotions, and cultural nuances that traditional methods miss completely.

Social listening focuses primarily on social media platforms and often includes engagement metrics and influencer identification. Brand mention tracking is broader, covering blogs, news sites, forums, review platforms, and any online content where your brand might be discussed.

While social listening tells you what's happening on social channels, comprehensive brand monitoring reveals how you're discussed in industry publications, competitor comparisons, and niche communities that don't appear in social feeds.

For straightforward text, AI sentiment analysis achieves 85-95% agreement with human analysts while processing thousands of documents in minutes versus weeks. Humans still outperform AI in extremely nuanced cases involving cultural references or industry-specific jargon.

The most effective approach combines AI automation for scale with human review for critical or ambiguous mentions. This hybrid model provides comprehensive coverage without sacrificing accuracy on important conversations.

Key brand health metrics include sentiment ratio (positive vs. negative mentions), mention volume trends, share of voice versus competitors, topic clustering (what people associate with your brand), and sentiment by channel or region.

These metrics reveal whether marketing campaigns are resonating, identify emerging customer concerns, and track competitive positioning over time. The most valuable insights often come from changes in these metrics rather than absolute numbers.

Daily monitoring is ideal for most businesses, with real-time alerts for negative sentiment spikes. High-profile brands or those in crisis situations may need hourly monitoring, while smaller businesses might find weekly monitoring sufficient.

The frequency should match your response capabilities—there's little value in hourly detection if your team can only respond daily. Start with daily monitoring and adjust based on volume and urgency of findings.
